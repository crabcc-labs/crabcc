# What Rust Was Saying To Itself: A Reading of the 2015-2017 Blog Anthology

## What this collection is

Brian Anderson — `brson` — kept a gist called *all-the-rust-blogs*, a hand-curated reading list of Rust blog posts from roughly Rust 1.0 (May 2015) through Rust ~1.20 (mid 2017). It is the canonical document of how the Rust community talked to itself during the language's first two years out of beta. There were 123 posts that survived the link-rot of the intervening decade well enough to be re-fetched, organised into chapters that mirror the language's structure: Ownership, Concurrency, Traits, `unsafe` Rust, Macros, Performance, Async I/O, Rust in Practice, Culture.

What strikes you reading the whole corpus end to end is how *settled* the technical arguments already were by the end of 2017. The borrow checker had won; `Send` and `Sync` had won; trait objects had stabilised into a fat-pointer ABI nobody was going to touch again; iterators had been benchmarked into the ground and found to be free. The fights that remained were marketing fights — what to put on the elevator pitch — and the long, careful argument about what `unsafe` *meant*, which Niko Matsakis and Ralf Jung were still working out and which would only resolve, properly, with Stacked Borrows in 2019 and Tree Borrows after that.

If you read this anthology now, in 2026, you are reading the founding documents of a language that has since become a dependency of approximately everything safety-critical you'd care to name. Most of what is taught about Rust today is taught in vocabulary these posts invented. The throughline of the era was not "we're proving the design"; it was "we've proved the design, now we need to teach it." Five big ideas got worked out, in roughly this order, and they're the spine of what follows.

A note before we start. The corpus is not balanced; it is opinionated, because brson curated it with a teacher's eye. Niko Matsakis appears nine times. Huon Wilson, eight. Manish Earth, seven. Llogiq, eleven. Herman Radtke, six. The voices belong to the people who were doing the language design work, the standard-library work, and the early ecosystem work, and whose blogs were the channel by which the rest of us learned what they had decided. This is the period — pre-Twitter-decay, pre-Discord, pre-RFC-bot — when the way you found out what was happening in Rust was to subscribe to about twenty RSS feeds. The corpus is what those feeds produced.

## The five big ideas the era converged on

### 1. Ownership as resource discipline, not just memory safety

The single most important pivot in this corpus is the moment Yehuda Katz publishes [Rust Means Never Having to Close a Socket](http://blog.skylight.io/rust-means-never-having-to-close-a-socket/) in late 2014, and a year and a half later you can see the entire ownership corpus — Manish Earth, Ricardo Martins, Chris Morgan, Rufflewind — all arguing the same thing in different registers: ownership is not a memory-safety hack. It is a generalised resource-management primitive that happens to also handle memory.

The clean form of the argument is in Manish Earth's [The Problem with Single-threaded Shared Mutability](https://manishearth.github.io/blog/2015/05/17/the-problem-with-shared-mutability/). Most readers in 2015 came to Rust assuming the borrow checker existed to prevent use-after-free in the C/C++ sense. Manish's post is the first widely-read piece to flip the framing: even in a single-threaded program with no `unsafe` and no concurrency, allowing two mutable aliases to the same enum lets you construct a segfault by re-tagging the discriminant while a reference into the payload is live. The borrow checker is enforcing a generalised read-write-lock discipline. The motivation is reasoning, not memory.

Once you see ownership through that lens, [Manish's *Wrapper Types in Rust*](https://manishearth.github.io/blog/2015/05/27/wrapper-types-in-rust-choosing-your-guarantees/) — folded into the official book within months of publication — falls out as the natural taxonomy. `Box<T>` is owned heap allocation. `Rc<T>` is shared-ownership without thread safety. `RefCell<T>` is single-threaded runtime-checked exterior immutability. `Mutex<T>` is `RefCell` plus thread safety. Every wrapper is "I'd like to relax one rule; please give me the cheapest way to do it." Ricardo Martins' three-part [interior-mutability series](https://ricardomartins.cc/2016/06/08/interior-mutability) is the application-level treatment of the same idea — `Rc::clone` *needs* interior mutability for its refcount, which is why `Cell<usize>` exists, which is why the language ships with the `UnsafeCell` primitive nobody is supposed to use directly.

The deepest version of this argument is the one almost nobody saw at the time: ownership generalises to file descriptors, locks, GPU buffers, transactional database handles, and (eventually) async cancellation tokens. Yehuda's 2014 post made this claim almost in passing — "this is true about virtually all resources in Rust" — and the rest of the corpus spent two years working out the consequences. By the time you reach Niko Matsakis' [Rayon post](http://smallcultfollowing.com/babysteps/blog/2015/12/18/rayon-data-parallelism-in-rust/), the language is using ownership-of-borrows to track which thread can see which slice of an array, and nobody flinches.

### 2. Fearless concurrency via `Send` / `Sync`

The corpus's purest piece of language design is the auto-trait machinery for thread safety. Two posts published within months of each other — Huon Wilson's [Some Notes on Send and Sync](https://huonw.github.io/blog/2015/02/some-notes-on-send-and-sync/) and Manish Earth's [How Rust Achieves Thread Safety](https://manishearth.github.io/blog/2015/05/30/how-rust-achieves-thread-safety/) — established the canonical definitions: `T: Send` means moving a `T` to another thread is safe, `T: Sync` means a `&T` can cross a thread boundary, and `T: Sync` iff `&T: Send`. Both traits are auto-implemented based on field types. Both are `unsafe` to implement manually because the compiler can't prove the invariants hold. That's it. That is the entire concurrency-safety story.

What makes this design extraordinary is how much it gets for free. The corpus's favourite demonstration: a `Sender<T>` for `T: Send` is itself `Send`, but a `Sender<Rc<U>>` is *not* `Send`, because `Rc` is `!Send`. So a single channel implementation, written once, is automatically usable cross-thread for thread-safe payloads and only thread-locally for non-thread-safe payloads. No "single-threaded channel" type. No runtime check. The type system computes the answer for you. Huon's [Defaulting to Thread-safety: Closures and Concurrency](https://huonw.github.io/blog/2015/05/defaulting-to-thread-safety/) closes the loop for closures: because closures desugar to anonymous structs whose fields are the captured environment, and `thread::spawn` requires `F: Send + 'static`, you literally cannot compile a `spawn` that captures a non-`Send` value. The compiler has all the information it needs, and the rule reads naturally.

The proof that this scales is Niko Matsakis' Rayon. The [original announcement post](http://smallcultfollowing.com/babysteps/blog/2015/12/18/rayon-data-parallelism-in-rust/) and the [three-part Parallel Iterators series](http://smallcultfollowing.com/babysteps/blog/2016/02/19/parallel-iterators-part-1-foundations/) build out a work-stealing scheduler whose entire safety argument reduces to: "the public API takes closures, the closures must be `Send`, the scheduler can't see your data otherwise." Once you trust `Send`/`Sync`, you trust `par_iter()`. The blog post benchmarks back this up — Rayon takes a sequential iterator chain, swaps `iter()` for `par_iter()`, and yields linear speedups. Eleven years later, this is still how the crate works.

The hidden reckoning in this corpus is Ralf Jung's [How MutexGuard was Sync When It Should Not Have Been](https://www.ralfj.de/blog/2017/06/09/mutexguard-sync.html). The standard library shipped with `MutexGuard<Cell<T>>: Sync` for years before formal verification (the RustBelt project) caught it. The model is simple. Implementing the model correctly across a real standard library is not. The corpus is candid about this — the design wins, but soundness is constant maintenance.

### 3. Trait objects and generics: a "pick-your-cost" abstraction surface

Huon Wilson's four-part trait-objects series — [Peeking Inside Trait Objects](https://huonw.github.io/blog/2015/01/peeking-inside-trait-objects/), [The Sized Trait](https://huonw.github.io/blog/2015/01/the-sized-trait/), [Object Safety](http://huonw.github.io/blog/2015/01/object-safety/), [Where Self meets Sized](https://huonw.github.io/blog/2015/05/where-self-meets-sized-revisiting-object-safety/) — is the corpus's most concentrated piece of teaching. Read in order, the four posts walk you through a single argument: traits are Rust's only mechanism for ad-hoc polymorphism, and the type system's job is to give you a knob between two costs. Generics monomorphise: each instantiation produces a fresh, inlinable copy of the function, with zero runtime overhead and a fatter binary. Trait objects type-erase: a `&dyn Foo` is a `(data, vtable)` fat pointer, a single function body, with one indirect call per method.

Once you see the fat-pointer layout, the ergonomics of object safety stop being arbitrary. A trait method with a generic parameter (`fn frobnicate<U>(&self, u: U)`) cannot live in a vtable, because the vtable would need an entry per `U`. A method that returns `Self` cannot live in a vtable, because the return slot's size depends on the erased type. The rules are not mysterious; they are forced by what a vtable can encode. Huon's post 4 — *Where Self Meets Sized* — adds the practical escape hatch: `where Self: Sized` on a method body opts that method out of the dyn-compatible API, lets you keep it for the generic API, and lets the trait still be object-safe. This single trick unlocks the design pattern used by half the iterator and stream traits in the modern stdlib.

The corpus complements Huon's deep dive with practitioner posts: llogiq's [Rust's Built-in Traits, the When, How & Why](https://llogiq.github.io/2015/07/30/traits.html) is the library author's checklist (derive `Debug` and `Default` always; implement `From` not bespoke `from_X` methods); Ben Ashford's [Rust Traits for Developer Friendly Libraries](https://benashford.github.io/blog/2015/05/24/rust-traits-for-developer-friendly-libraries/) is the case study of building an Elasticsearch client in Rust where every query DSL combinator is a trait. The throughline is "default to generics, reach for `dyn` when you need heterogeneity or smaller code." That rule of thumb has not changed.

The era's stranger trait posts — Stephen Leffler's [Rust's Type System is Turing-Complete](https://sdleffler.github.io/RustTypeSystemTuringComplete/), llogiq's [Type-level Shenanigans](https://llogiq.github.io/2015/12/12/types.html), Manish's [What are Sum, Product, and Pi types?](https://manishearth.github.io/blog/2017/03/04/what-are-sum-product-and-pi-types/) — are the era's playful frontier. Most of what they encode by hand (Peano numerals as nested generic structs, type-level lists as cons cells) was supplanted by const generics and GATs. They are valuable now mostly as a record of how *enthusiastic* the community was about exploring the type system before the practical answers arrived.

The conversion-traits sub-thread deserves its own paragraph. Llogiq's [Where Are You From::from?](https://llogiq.github.io/2015/11/27/from-into.html) and Ricardo Martins' [Convenient and Idiomatic Conversions in Rust](https://ricardomartins.cc/2016/08/03/convenient_and_idiomatic_conversions_in_rust) converge on the same library-author rule: never invent your own `from_x` or `to_y` methods when `From<T>`/`Into<T>` will do. The blanket `impl<T, U: From<T>> Into<U> for T` means a single `From` implementation gives you the `Into` direction for free, and the orphan rule (which the corpus complains is underdocumented) is what keeps this from collapsing into ambiguity. This pattern is one of the most under-credited reasons Rust's library ecosystem composes well — every crate's types speak a common conversion vocabulary, and `?` for error conversion in Rust 2018 is just `From::from` applied at the right desugared spot.

### 4. `unsafe` as a contract, not a license — the niko/ralfj evolution

The most intellectually concentrated thread in the corpus is the three-year argument about what `unsafe` means. Huon's 2014 [What Does Rust's `unsafe` Mean?](https://huonw.github.io/blog/2014/07/what-does-rusts-unsafe-mean/) is the founding statement: the keyword grants five capabilities (deref raw pointers, mutate statics, call `unsafe fn`, implement `unsafe trait`, access union fields), and the price is that you must uphold the invariants the borrow checker would have enforced. The keyword is a contract, not a license to do anything.

Ralf Jung's [The Scope of Unsafe](https://www.ralfj.de/blog/2016/01/09/the-scope-of-unsafe.html) sharpens this in a way that genuinely surprised most readers in 2016: a *safe* function in the same module as an `unsafe` block can break safety even though it has no `unsafe` keyword anywhere. The example is `Vec`: a private `set_len` can break `pop` even though `pop` is safe. The abstraction boundary is privacy, not the keyword. To review an `unsafe` block you must read every safe function with private access to the same fields. This claim sits behind every modern "unsafe-soundness review" and the entire `cargo-careful` / MIRI culture.

Niko Matsakis then spends a year working on what eventually becomes the unsafe code guidelines. His [Unsafe Abstractions](http://smallcultfollowing.com/babysteps/blog/2016/05/23/unsafe-abstractions/) post in May 2016 frames `unsafe` as a "C extension to Ruby" — local plug-ins that must present a safe API at their boundary. The follow-up, [The "Tootsie Pop" Model for Unsafe Code](http://smallcultfollowing.com/babysteps/blog/2016/05/27/the-tootsie-pop-model-for-unsafe-code/), proposes that the compiler be conservative inside an `unsafe` abstraction boundary and aggressive outside it. Three months later he publishes [the followup](http://smallcultfollowing.com/babysteps/blog/2016/08/18/tootsie-pop-followup/) candidly retracting the model — the `vec.get_unchecked(i)` example would make code *slower* after you reach for `unsafe` to skip a bounds check, which is the exact opposite of what users expect. The retraction is one of the most valuable artefacts in the corpus: it is Rust language design happening in public, with errata, on a six-month iteration cycle.

The thread closes with [Assigning Blame to Unsafe Code](http://smallcultfollowing.com/babysteps/blog/2017/01/22/assigning-blame-to-unsafe-code/), where Niko commits to "executable guidelines" — whatever the eventual rules turn out to be, they must be testable by a sanitiser that aborts on UB. He cites blame-calculus from gradual-typing research as the framework. This is the design that ships as MIRI within 18 months and that becomes Stacked Borrows / Tree Borrows. The corpus does not contain the resolution. It contains the argument that produced the resolution.

A small but important sub-thread: leaks are not unsafety. Huon's [Memory Leaks are Memory Safe](https://huonw.github.io/blog/2016/04/memory-leaks-are-memory-safe/) and Niko's [On Reference Counting and Leaks](http://smallcultfollowing.com/babysteps/blog/2015/04/29/on-reference-counting-and-leaks/) (the post-1.0 `JoinGuard` drama) settle the formal point: `mem::forget` is safe, leaks are a correctness/resource bug, and any API that requires "destructors will run" for soundness is the API's bug. This is the pre-1.0 mistake that taught the language its discipline.

### 5. Zero-cost abstractions, in practice

The most empirically grounded section of the corpus is the one that proves "zero-cost" by inspection of disassembly. The single best post is Ruud van Asseldonk's [Zero-cost abstractions](https://ruudvanasseldonk.com/2016/11/30/zero-cost-abstractions), which takes a 12-coefficient FIR filter inner loop in his FLAC decoder, writes it as an idiomatic iterator chain — `coefficients.iter().zip(window).map(|(c, s)| c * s).sum::<i64>() >> shift` — and shows that LLVM emits AVX2-vectorised code identical to what a hand-tuned C inner loop would produce. Once you have seen this once, every other "iterators are fast" claim in the corpus becomes easy to believe.

The trophy is Andrew Gallant's [ripgrep is faster than {grep, ag, git grep, ucg, pt, sift}](http://blog.burntsushi.net/ripgrep/) — 25 benchmarks, three architectural insights (lazy DFA construction, SIMD literal scanning, the surprising claim that memory-mapped IO is *slower* than `read` for searching many small files), and a level of attention to fairness that the rest of the benchmarking literature should aspire to. The post does double duty as a benchmark report and as the architecture deep-dive on the regex crate. It is the single most-cited "Rust ate this niche" trophy of the entire era and it has only become more true.

The async story belongs in the same column. Aaron Turon's [Zero-cost Futures in Rust](http://aturon.github.io/blog/2016/08/11/futures/) and [Designing Futures for Rust](http://aturon.github.io/blog/2016/09/07/futures-design/) make the manifesto explicit: a `Future` trait based on demand-driven `poll() -> Async<T>` desugars to a state machine that, after monomorphisation, compiles to the same code mio users were writing by hand. Cancellation is dropping. Backpressure is the producer returning `NotReady`. Error propagation is `Result` baked into the trait. Karol Kuczmarski's [Asynchronous Rust for Fun and Profit](http://xion.io/post/programming/rust-async-closer-look.html), written about a year later, is candid that the ergonomics are still rough — combinator chains produce 800-character types — but the model is right. The async/await stabilisation in 2019 changed the syntax, not the model.

The cautionary companions to these wins are llogiq's [Rust Performance Pitfalls](https://llogiq.github.io/2017/06/01/perf-pitfalls.html) and Robert Grosse's [Optimizing Rc Memory Usage in Rust](https://medium.com/@robertgrosse/optimizing-rc-memory-usage-in-rust-6652de9e119e). "Zero cost" doesn't mean "no work to do." It means "the abstraction doesn't bleed cost; representation engineering still pays." Both lessons travel. Llogiq's pitfalls list — forgot `--release`, naïve `String` allocations in tight loops, `Box<dyn Trait>` where a generic would have monomorphised, `RefCell` borrows in hot paths, `format!()` instead of `write!()` — reads like a code-review checklist still useful today; Grosse's case study reaches for a custom tagged-pointer scheme to compress an eight-variant enum, demonstrating that the gap between "fine" and "great" performance still requires representation engineering even in a language whose default layouts are already tight.

Jack Mott's [Does Your Code Leave a Trail of Slowness?](https://jackmott.github.io/2017/02/27/trail-of-slow.html) is the corpus's most under-loved performance essay. Mott's claim — modern CPUs are RAM-latency bound, contiguous arrays beat linked lists by 10-100x, the *layout* of your data dominates the *speed* of your algorithm — is the unstated thesis underneath everything else. Rust's `Vec` + value-by-default semantics produce densely packed memory; Java's `ArrayList<Box<T>>` produces a pointer chase per element. This is the layer at which Rust's performance story is structural, not optimisational. The corpus assumes this and Mott states it cleanly.

## Two tonal threads

Two side-currents run through the corpus that are not technical but are worth flagging because they tell you something about the community.

The first is the *fireflower* debate. Steve Klabnik's December 2016 post *Rust is more than safety* triggered a week of responses cataloged in brson's [Fireflowers](https://brson.github.io/fireflowers/). The argument was about marketing: should the elevator pitch lead with safety, or with productivity, or with fearlessness, or with citizenship? Graydon Hoare himself weighed in with [Rust is Mostly Safety](http://graydon2.dreamwidth.org/247406.html), pushing back hard — safety in the systems space *is* Rust's reason for existing, and selling it on something else is selling something else. Nick Cameron's [Rust is About Productivity](http://www.ncameron.org/blog/rust-is-about-productivity/) and Anowell's [Rust is My Magic Whistle](http://anowell.com/posts/why-rust.html) made the productivity case. Hisako1337, kasma1990, and a dozen reddit threads added their framings. None of the participants disagreed about the language. They disagreed about which part to put in the elevator. Reading it now, the most interesting thing about the debate is how civil it was — the community was big enough by late 2016 to have a public, multi-author argument about its own identity and ship anyway.

The second is the *RIIR* maximalism. Robert O'Callahan's [Rewrite Everything In Rust](http://robert.ocallahan.org/2016/02/rewrite-everything-in-rust.html) is the maximalist version: glibc had a critical DNS vulnerability, the world has a memory-safety crisis, the moral argument for migration is overwhelming. Tony Arcieri's [It's Time for a Memory Safety Intervention](https://tonyarcieri.com/it-s-time-for-a-memory-safety-intervention) is the soberer cousin. Dan Connolly's [Have You Considered Rewriting it In Rust?](http://transitiontech.ca/random/RIIR) is the affectionate parody — the speaker is a Rust evangelist who finds excuses to rewrite his JavaScript build scripts in Rust. The simultaneous existence of the meme, the parody, and the real engineering effort (ripgrep displacing grep, parts of curl, parts of the kernel — much of it post-corpus, but predicted by it) is the most accurate snapshot of how the community talked about itself in 2016: missionary and self-aware in the same paragraph.

A third, smaller current: graydon's voice. [Things Rust Shipped Without](http://graydon2.dreamwidth.org/218040.html) and [Five Lists of Six Things About Rust](http://graydon2.dreamwidth.org/214016.html) are short, terse, and unsentimental. Graydon Hoare designed the language and then *left* the project before 1.0 — his retrospective posts read with the dispassion of someone who has no remaining political stake. The bullet list of what Rust shipped without (null pointers, array overruns, data races, wild pointers, uninitialised addressable memory, unions that allow access to the wrong field, GOTOs, header files, GC, type inference of integer literals) is one of the most quoted lines in the corpus, and the lightning-talk format is a model of compression. If you want to know what the original designer thought the language was *for*, read these two posts.

## What aged well, what rotted, what's missing

What aged well, in roughly the order it would appear in a 2026 onboarding curriculum: the borrow checker pedagogy, the `Send`/`Sync` model, RAII as resource management, `Result`-and-`?` error handling, ownership-as-reasoning, the fat-pointer trait-object ABI, the "default to generics" rule of thumb, demand-driven futures, ripgrep, zero-cost iterator chains, MIRI's executable-semantics philosophy, the newcomer-friendly contributor culture. None of these have been replaced. Most have been reinforced by a decade of evidence.

What rotted: `error-chain`, the era's dominant error-handling crate, was replaced by `failure`, which was replaced by `anyhow` plus `thiserror`. Hand-rolled futures combinators (`AndThen<MapErr<Then<...>>>`) were replaced wholesale by `async`/`await`. The 2015-era hygiene workarounds for `macro_rules!` (`$crate::`, `local_inner_macros`) were superseded by edition-aware import resolution. Most of the 2015 type-level-trickery posts predate const generics and GATs and look ingenious but unnecessary now. `mem::transmute` patterns for type-punning were largely replaced by `MaybeUninit` and stable bit-cast intrinsics. The Tootsie Pop model itself was retracted by its own author.

What's missing — the conspicuous absences in a 2026 reading: there is essentially no discussion of WebAssembly (Servo and asm.js were on the radar, wasm was not yet). There is no Cargo workspace material, no sccache/sccloud, no rustup. There is no async ecosystem fragmentation discussion (Tokio vs. async-std vs. smol — none of those crates existed yet). There are no procedural-macro-driven web frameworks. The corpus captures the language's foundations and almost none of its eventual ecosystem. That is exactly what makes it valuable: read it, and you see the design without the accumulated cruft.

## The reading list, by Sunday

Five posts to start with, in this order, each one paragraph.

1. **[Rust Means Never Having to Close a Socket](http://blog.skylight.io/rust-means-never-having-to-close-a-socket/) — Yehuda Katz.** Read this first. It is the post that made Rust make sense to a generation of garbage-collected-language programmers. The thesis — that ownership is general resource management, not just memory management — is the single most important reframing in the corpus, and Katz lands it in 4000 patient words.

2. **[The Problem with Single-threaded Shared Mutability](https://manishearth.github.io/blog/2015/05/17/the-problem-with-shared-mutability/) — Manish Earth.** Read this second. It explains *why* the borrow checker enforces what it enforces, with a single-page proof that aliased mutation in a single thread can construct a segfault by re-tagging an enum. Once you absorb this, the rest of the borrow-checker corpus is annotation.

3. **[Some Notes on Send and Sync](https://huonw.github.io/blog/2015/02/some-notes-on-send-and-sync/) — Huon Wilson.** Read this third. Two pages, the cleanest definition of the auto-trait machinery you will find anywhere, and the single load-bearing demonstration that `Sender<T>` is `Send` iff `T` is. After this, every concurrency post in the corpus is application of the same template.

4. **[The Tootsie Pop Model for Unsafe Code](http://smallcultfollowing.com/babysteps/blog/2016/05/27/the-tootsie-pop-model-for-unsafe-code/) (and the followup) — Niko Matsakis.** Read this fourth — but read both the original and the [retraction](http://smallcultfollowing.com/babysteps/blog/2016/08/18/tootsie-pop-followup/). It is the only place in the corpus where you watch a Rust language designer publish a model, take community feedback, and change his mind in print. It is also the cleanest evidence that the unsafe code guidelines are an open design problem, not a settled standard.

5. **[ripgrep is faster than {grep, ag, git grep, ucg, pt, sift}](http://blog.burntsushi.net/ripgrep/) — Andrew Gallant.** Read this fifth, last, on a Sunday afternoon with coffee. It is the longest post in the corpus and the most rewarding, because by the time you reach it you will have absorbed the language design well enough to read it as evidence — a 25-benchmark proof that everything the previous four posts promised is empirically true. After this, you have read the era.

Read with these five, the corpus reads as a single argument, made by a community that knew what it was doing, in a window of time the rest of us are still living downstream of.
